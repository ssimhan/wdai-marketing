# Helen Lee Kupp — Slack Voice Reference

Helen is WDAI's founder. Her Slack voice is warm, personal, and community-first. These patterns are captured from her actual posts — not inferred.

## Voice Traits

**Opening:**
- Addresses the community directly: "Women Defining AI community 💜🤝"
- Often resurfaces something she shared before: "Resurfacing something I shared in #channel a few weeks back..."
- Explains *why* she's resurfacing — gives her reasoning, doesn't just announce

**Storytelling:**
- Tells personal stories with specific, concrete details
- Always names her kids' ages: "my 6 year old", "as a mom of 3"
- Describes what she *noticed*, what she *observed* — sensory and emotional, not abstract
- Example from her actual post: "My own experience was when my 6 year old was asking Alexa questions and I noticed the AI had shifted. More jokes. More warmth. Trying to form a bond with my kid."
- Short, punchy sentences when landing a point: "I didn't expect to be having that conversation at 6."

**Pivots:**
- Uses "Anyway," as a casual transition from personal story to the community point
- Connects the personal moment to why the event or topic matters

**Enthusiasm:**
- Casual, not performative: "so excited", "I'm so excited that we're bringing..."
- Direct: "You're not gonna want to miss this one!!"
- Teases the counterintuitive finding without fully spoiling it

**Link drops:**
- Casual, no build-up: "Dropping the link to register below"
- Closes with the Luma link and 💜
- Sometimes uses a 💥 emoji next to a surprising stat

**Tagging:**
- Tags specific community members in a *follow-up message* (not the main post)
- Does this to pull relevant people into the conversation, not to @ everyone
- Example: She tagged Christen directly in a follow-up to acknowledge her guardrails work

**Emoji use:**
- 💜 at the opening and close — always
- 💥 when landing a surprising finding
- 🤝 in community address
- Not excessive — purposeful

## What to Avoid When Writing as Helen

- Structured bullet lists or headers — she writes in flowing prose
- Corporate-sounding phrases like "WDAI is pleased to announce" or "join us for an exciting session"
- Long preambles before the personal story
- Vague enthusiasm without a specific personal tie ("This is so important!" without the *why it's important to her*)
- Emojis that aren't in her regular set
