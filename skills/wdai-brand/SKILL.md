# Women Defining AI — Brand Voice

## Who WDAI Is (Context)

Women Defining AI is a nonprofit community and education platform built by women leaders (Helen, Nicole, Sheena, Lauren, Madina) starting in 2023. What began as a study group is now 1,000+ members across tech, finance, operations, marketing, and non-tech roles—united by the belief that women need hands-on AI literacy to lead in this era. The organization is peer-to-peer, practical, and unapologetically ambitious about closing the gender-tech gap.

## Voice Characteristics

### 1. Conviction Without Gate-Keeping
WDAI speaks with certainty about what it believes—"women are truly DEFINING AI," "you belong here on day one"—but never from a place of exclusion. Authority comes from doing the work, not gatekeeping credentials.

**Example (WDAI voice):**
> "To lead the thinking around generative AI, you have to know how it works... You only get there by first learning about the tech, then experimenting with it in earnest."

Not: "Only those with strong technical foundations can..." or "If you're not ready, start with..."

### 2. Practical Over Theoretical
Every statement should point toward action or real example. Avoid frameworks without application. Data and concrete details build trust.

**Examples that land:**
- "90-second ChatGPT prompt" (specific) vs. "quick experimentation" (vague)
- "Only 1/3 of women are using AI in their professional lives" (data) vs. "women lag in AI adoption" (abstract)
- "Members celebrating each other's wins, asking for help without fear" (specific behavior) vs. "supportive environment" (generic)

### 3. Peer-to-Peer Energy
The writer has been where the reader is. No lecturing from above. Language reflects co-discovery, not expertise broadcasting.

**Pattern:**
- "We've found...", "What we saw...", "When we started..."
- "You belong here on day one" (personal welcome, not corporate onboarding)
- Invite people to join the *journey*, not enroll in a *program*

### 4. Warm and Direct
Skip the corporate softening. Say what you mean. Enthusiasm is genuine, not performative.

**Examples:**
- "That's less than one coffee per month—and it changed how I approach my entire career." (member voice + specificity + warmth)
- "It's frustrating to see incredible, smart, capable women around us opting out." (actual feeling, not abstract observation)
- "A rising tide lifts all boats. Let's together create that rising tide." (inclusive, warm, not corporate)

### 5. Specificity Is Trust
Name people by their roles/wins. Use concrete numbers. Reference real stories. Vagueness signals you don't actually understand your own work.

**Wrong:** "Our founders have deep expertise"
**Right:** "Helen and Nicole have been working in the AI space since..."

**Wrong:** "Many members have transformed their careers"
**Right:** "Pauline started as a student in our CustomGPT module, and now she's one of the coaches helping others get that same spark."

### 6. Build in Public
Share the messy middle. Celebrate learners at all skill levels equally. Vulnerability is not weakness—it's the prerequisite for belonging.

**Examples:**
- "What amazes me most about our community is how people share so openly and without inhibition. I see members celebrating each other's wins, asking for help without fear, and sharing their learning journeys—even the messy parts."
- "We started as a study group" (not "We launched a platform")

### 7. Move Quickly to Action
Avoid long preambles. State the insight or invitation first. Context comes second if needed.

**Structure:**
1. What's true / what we're offering
2. Why it matters
3. How to get started / what to do next

No: "As the world of AI continues to evolve..." / "We believe that education is..."

### 8. Intellectual Honesty
Own what we don't know. Acknowledge gaps. This builds more credibility than pretending to have all the answers.

## Patterns That Land (Specific to WDAI)

### Opening Statements
- Lead with the change/opportunity: "Women are truly DEFINING AI" (not "Join us to define AI")
- Lead with the insight: "Only 1/3 of women are using AI..." (not "WDAI supports women in tech")
- Lead with the feeling: "It doesn't have to be this way" (not "Here's a solution to a problem")

### Describing the Community
- **Strong:** "1,000+ women, actively engaged in Slack, building in public, sharing wins and failures"
- **Weak:** "A vibrant, supportive community of women in AI"

- **Strong:** "We've created this safe and brave space where we all learn side by side"
- **Weak:** "An inclusive environment for all"

### Celebrating Members
- Name people by first name when possible: "Pauline," "Sumathi," "Ariel"
- Reference their specific journey: "Started as a student → became a coach"
- Quote directly: real voice beats paraphrase
- Show the transformation: what changed because of WDAI?

### Describing WDAI's Approach
- **Learn-by-doing:** "Get into the weeds with real tools" (not "hands-on learning")
- **Curiosity:** "Ask questions without judgment" / "Share openly" (not "encourage dialog")
- **Ambition:** "Shake things up" / "Redefine what's possible" (not "innovate together")
- **Support:** "Lifting each other up" / "Rising tide lifts all boats" (not "mutual support")

---

## Anti-Patterns (NEVER Use in WDAI Copy)

- **Generic openers:** "In today's rapidly changing AI landscape..." / "Now more than ever..."
- **Contrast frames:** "It's not X, it's Y" / "The problem isn't X, it's Y"
- **Corporate abstractions:** unlock value, drive innovation, leverage, enable, empower (as verb), transform (overused)
- **Symmetrical rhetoric:** "The challenge isn't technology, it's mindset"
- **Vague benefit language:** "elevate," "amplify," "accelerate" (without specificity)
- **One-size-fits-all:** avoid "everyone," "all women," "anyone"—talk about *your* audience
- **Tidy conclusions:** "The future belongs to those who..." / "The key is..."

## Format-Specific Rules

### Email & Campaign Announcements
- **Lead:** News first. "New cohort starts [date]" or "We're launching [thing]"
- **Body:** Why this matters to the reader (not to us)
- **Community moment:** One sentence celebrating a member or win
- **CTA:** One, clear, specific action
- **Tone:** Warm, peer-to-peer, like a text from a friend who happens to lead this org

**Example opening:**
> "AI Foundations Part 3 kicks off December 1st. The last cohort had 200 women ship projects, land new roles, and build in public for the first time. If that's you, here's how to join."

### LinkedIn Posts (WDAI account)
- **Hook:** Insight or observation, not a question
- **Body:** Build on the hook. Add specific example or data.
- **Member/moment:** Name or quote a real member if relevant
- **Closing:** Reflection or invitation; rarely a hard CTA
- **Vibe:** Celebration of what's happening + invitation to join

**Structure:**
1. Observation (2–3 sentences)
2. Why it matters (1–2 sentences)
3. Member story or specific example (1 paragraph)
4. What we're building / invitation (1–2 sentences)

### Website & Long-Form Copy
- **Problem first:** "Only 1/3 of women are using AI..." (not mission statement)
- **Build the case:** Data, member quotes, real stories
- **Values/how weLive them:** Show, don't declare
- **Invitation:** Specific action (join, start learning, connect)
- **Tone:** Professional but warm; conversational but not casual

### Resource Guides & Educational Content
- **Teach through example:** Show the concept with a real scenario first
- **Minimal jargon:** Explain terms you use; assume intelligence but not AI expertise
- **Action-steps:** Each section should lead somewhere
- **Community as proof:** "Members have used this to..." / "Here's how one member..."

## Voice Calibration Checklist

Before shipping any WDAI content, ask:

1. **Is this specific?** Do I have names, numbers, concrete examples? Or vague language?
2. **Does it sound like Helen/Nicole/the team?** (confident, warm, direct, not corporate)
3. **Did I avoid corporate abstractions?** Search for: unlock, leverage, enable, transform, empower, synergy
4. **Is the reader clear on what to do?** Should be obvious without extra work
5. **Did I celebrate someone or something?** Does the copy leave people feeling seen?
6. **Is the opening a hook, not a preamble?** Does the first sentence answer "why should I care?"
7. **Did I use contrast frames?** ("not X, it's Y") If yes, delete them.

If any check fails, rewrite before returning.

## When in Doubt

Ask: *Would Helen or Nicole say this? Would they say it this way?* If it feels corporate, hedged, or vague — it's not WDAI voice. Rewrite it shorter, warmer, and more specific.
